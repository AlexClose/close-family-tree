⚜ The Close Family Story

From a German valley to America — 400 years in seven villages

The 1600s — The Beginning

The Eckels of Niederalben

The story of your family in this corner of Germany begins around 1600, in a small village called Niederalben in the Kusel district of what was then the Holy Roman Empire. Here, a man named Abraham Eckels was born around the year 1600. He is the oldest confirmed ancestor in your family tree.

His son, Hannss Eckel, born in 1628, rose to become the Schultheiß of Niederalben — the village magistrate, essentially the mayor and judge rolled into one. This was a position of significant community trust. Hannss lived through some of the most turbulent times in German history, including the aftermath of the Thirty Years War, and died in 1708 at the age of 80.

In Niederalben:
Abraham Eckels (~1600–1646) → Hannss Eckel, Schultheiß (1628–1708) → Johann Abraham Eckel (1652–1700)

Early 1700s — The Family Expands

Neighboring Villages, Intertwining Lives

In the early 1700s, the family spread slightly across the small valley. Johann Friedrich Eckel was born in 1691 in nearby Sankt Julian, just a few miles from Niederalben. His daughter Maria Catharina Eckel was born there in 1721.

At the same time, in the village of Ulmet, another family was putting down roots — the Drumm family. Johann Georg Drumm was born in Ulmet in 1717. These two families — the Eckels and the Drumms — would eventually be joined through marriage, weaving together the bloodlines that would eventually produce the Close family.

Meanwhile in Sankt Julian, a woman named Maria Barbara Neu was born in 1755. She would become the mother of one of the most important figures in your family story.

In Sankt Julian: Johann Friedrich Eckel (1691) · Maria Catharina Eckel (1721) · Maria Barbara Neu (1755)
In Ulmet: Johann Georg Drumm (1717) · Johann Sebastian Drumm (1753)
✦ ✦ ✦
Late 1700s — The Marriage That Started It All

The Clos and Drumm Families Unite

On 7 February 1803, in the town of Kusel, a young couple were married. He was Johann Christian Clos, born in 1779 in Bavaria, living in Rammelsbach. She was Anna Maria Drumm, born in 1784 in the village of Erdesbach — daughter of Johann Sebastian Drumm of Ulmet and Maria Barbara Neu of Sankt Julian.

This marriage is the most important event in your German family history. We have the actual marriage record confirming the date and place. Through this union, the Eckel, Drumm, Neu and Clos bloodlines all came together. Every Close family member in America today descends from this couple.

Johann Christian Clos lived in Rammelsbach until his death in 1846. Anna Maria died two years later in 1848. They never saw America — but their children would.

Married 7 February 1803, Kusel:
Johann Christian Clos (1779–1846, Rammelsbach) + Anna Maria Drumm (1784–1848, Erdesbach)
→ Son: Johann Gottfried Clos, born ~1805 in Altenglan

Mid 1800s — The Decision to Leave

America Calls — The Clos Family Emigrates

The mid-1800s were a time of great upheaval in the German states. Political revolutions in 1848, economic hardship, and limited opportunities for working families drove millions of Germans to look westward. The Clos family of Altenglan and Rammelsbach were among them.

Johann Gottfried Clos, born around 1805 in Altenglan, made the decision to emigrate. He and his family — including his son Friederich Clos, born in 1830 — eventually made their way to Bay City, Michigan, where a thriving German immigrant community had already established itself in the booming lumber industry town.

The last family member actually born in the old country was Frederic Clos Jr., born on 3 October 1850 in Mühlbach an der Glan — the tiny village at the heart of your ancestral homeland. He was likely just a young child or infant when the family made the crossing.

1874 — A New Life in Michigan

Bay City, Michigan — The Lumber Capital of the World

By 1874, Frederic Close Jr. was a 24-year-old laborer living in Bay City, Michigan — which was at that time one of the most productive lumber centers in the entire world. Millions of board feet of timber flowed down the Saginaw River through Bay City every year, and immigrant workers like Frederic found steady work in the sawmills.

On 17 November 1874, Frederic married Mary Grischke — a young German woman also from the immigrant community. Fascinatingly, Mary's family name, Grischke, appears elsewhere in your family tree: Ernestine Grischke and Henry Grischke were also from the same German immigrant community in Bay City. The Grischke and Close/Clos families may have known each other back in Germany, or met through the tight-knit German community in Bay City.

By 1880 the family was established — Frederic was working as a Mill Hand, and he and Mary had two young daughters, Bertha and Maggie. Their son Frederick John Close — your great-great-grandfather — was born in Bay City in 1881.

Married Bay City, 17 Nov 1874: Frederic Close Jr. + Mary Grischke
1880 Census: Mill Hand, Bay City 3rd Ward
Children: 11 total including Frederick John Close (1881) — your ancestor
Died: 25 Sep 1909, Bay City — buried after 35 years in America
🌍 The Full Journey: From Hannss Eckel serving as village magistrate in Niederalben in the 1600s, through six generations of families living within walking distance of each other in the Kusel valley, to Frederic Close Jr. crossing the Atlantic in the 1850s and working in the Michigan lumber mills — this is the story of how your family became American.

The villages of Niederalben, Sankt Julian, Ulmet, Erdesbach, Rammelsbach, Altenglan and Mühlbach an der Glan are all still there today, largely unchanged, just a few miles apart in the rolling hills of Rhineland-Palatinate. Your family's story is written in the church records of those parishes — waiting to be found.
The Next Generation

From Bay City to Philadelphia — and Beyond

Frederick John Close, born 17 October 1881 in Bay City Michigan, was the first generation of the Close family born entirely in America. He married Lilly Frances Hannah Hartzell and their son Lewis Frederick Close was born on 11 June 1910 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania — the family had moved east.

Lewis married Catherine Maria Wormeck in Philadelphia, and their son Richard Emerson Close — your father — was born in 1948. And on that line, you — Alexander Kenneth Close — were born in 2001.

Five generations from Mühlbach an der Glan to you. The journey your ancestors began when they left that small Bavarian village eventually led here.
